We are a priveleged few to live in a time that wikipedia exists. It is a anthropogenic miracle, one of the wonders of the world.

Onezoom.org

 Why is this one of your favorite sites? Onezoom is a website that takes a unique approach to displaying phylogenetic tree data in an interactive and aesthetically engaging way--a problem that I am keenly interested in. Most phylogenetic displays are not zoomable and pannable, and onezoom also has found a way to make the experience visually compelling. I think their solution has some sharp limitations (no trait-mapping, limited manipulability, limited information display), but this is a challenging design problem, and I think what they've done is pretty cool. I find it's successes and limitations extremely helpful, as I'm currently experimenting with more complete solutions.

What area of the site is your eye drawn to when looking at the homepage? Stand back further, what area is your eye drawn to now? Is that area the most important area of the site? It actually sort of depends on which option you choose for the 'fractal shape' option. The default is a curly clockwise spiral which leads the eye around from branch to branch. This scales nicely as you zoom in on a very speciose taxon, but one problem I have with it is that it seems to visually imply an ordering among the taxa on a branch--which is arbitrary and not biologically real. One simple solution they could offer is a button to 'reshuffle' the ordering of the branches--to remind a relatively naive viewer that the ordering is in fact arbitrary. Even cooler would be you could click to drag branches and have them kind of spring back into some sort of shape, but to allow the user to rearrange the shape of the branches. This is why I like using force-diagrams, although obviously my solution is still very primitive

How would you describe the website visually? List 5 adjectives. (i.e. "pretty", "elegant", "simple", "dark", "cluttered", "basic" etc)
  • Dynamic (the whole point is that it's a zoomable, manipulable design solution)
  • Elegant
  • Clean
  • Bright
  • Simple


 What problem does this website solve? What content does it have? The basic problem the site tries to solve is making datasets about phylogenetic relationships visually intuitive and explorable. They have pretty good datasets of tetrapods and green plants, although that's about it. This is actually a bit surprising. What is their data management solution like, that it isn't really easy to just add more archived data? A solution to this problem should be scalable. I wonder why they haven't expanded more.

What 5 adjectives would you use to describe the content, focus, and purpose of the site? (i.e. "practical", "fun", "whimsical", "silly", "serious" etc) How does that compare to the adjectives you used to describe the site visually?
  • Educational (in a kind of minimal way that just presents the data in an explorable, self-explanatory way)
  • Intuitive (as in, it is an attempt to use design to make something that is not intuitive--a phylogenetic dataset--visually comprehensible).
  • limited--onezoom represents a one-off experiment in phylogenetic tree visualization, rather than an evolving, iterative approach to tackling this problem.
  • Cutesy (this is not a good thing. I think the idea of a phylogenetic tree being depicted as literally like a plant has its limitations).
  • Ummm, phylogenetic? I couldn't think of another adjective.
 How easy is it to find what you are looking for from the homepage? How about from another page? Meh at best. Search functionality is not great, and even the search bar itself is hidden among a set of inscrutable buttons along a head-bar, that you have to hover over to see what they do.

 How easy is it to browse through all the content of the site? Fairly easy. Despite the fact that you have tens of thousands of taxa in their database, what onezoom does well is the dynamic zoom and pan, allowing you to quickly traverse this dataset using 2d graphic space as a proxy medium for phylogenetic relationships, a la the Darwinian conception.

How do you feel after being on the site for a while? (i.e. "bored", "happy", "anxious or hyper", "like I wasted a lot of time" etc)
  • Inspired--to improve on their attempt,
  • frustrated--at the limitations of the site,
    • Really, I've never spent too much time browsing the site, compared to just cruising the phylogenetic data on even a visually static (and way crappier in a design senes) site like tolweb.org, because the latter has more actual info to explore (although tolweb kind of sucks because a lot of their tree data is super out of date--they must have implemented a really crappy database solution, if its so hard to update). Honestly, Wikipedia is still a better way to learn about phylogenetic relationships, because each taxonomic node has lots of extra information, so there's something to actually learn as you browse other than just a bunch of disembodies names arrayed in space--where most of the spatial organization is not actually informative.

Does the site sell anything? If so, have you purchased any of it? Why or why not? No I think it was paid for by a limited grant or something, which probably has a lot to do with why it hasn't been improved much beyond the original one-trick functionality.

howlt.com

 Why is this one of your favorite sites? Well, I didn't know about this website until today, but the other sites I picked to blog about, I picked because of their content/function, without much regard for design aspects (except in a very functional sense). I discovered howlt.com via awwwards.com's list of best webistes. Really, this site breaks a lot of my general rules for what i consider good websites: I generally think bells and whistles should be kept to a minimum, and this website is basically just a collection of bells and whistles (it even starts playing music automatically on page-load, which seems like a terrible idea generally). However, it is a SUPER skillfully put together collection of bells and whistles, and it comes together for a really fun experience. navigating the website is like playing a game, and that is a really powerful thing to achieve. I also just think I can learn a lot of cool techniques by exploring the css and javascript for the site.

What area of the site is your eye drawn to when looking at the homepage? Stand back further, what area is your eye drawn to now? Is that area the most important area of the site? The site is animated upon loading, with some weird blimp thing floating across the screen, and clouds, and weird little cartoony icons and things. it is somewhat visually confusing, but in an engaging, exploration-inviting way. As I noted already, this seems to me to totally break many of the rules of good web design, but in a deliberate and powerful way that can be very informative to study.
